Project Overview

Credit risk prediction is a fundamental problem in the financial sector, where lenders must assess the likelihood that a borrower will default on a loan. Accurate credit risk assessment is essential to minimize financial losses while ensuring fair access to credit.

Two datasets are used in this study: an Indian bureau-style credit dataset representing traditional lending systems, and a US peer-to-peer lending dataset reflecting market-driven credit behavior.

Models Used

Two machine learning models were implemented to capture different characteristics of credit risk.

  • Logistic Regression: A linear and interpretable baseline model widely used in regulated financial environments due to its transparency and stability.
  • Random Forest: An ensemble learning model capable of capturing non-linear relationships and complex borrower behavior present in real-world credit data.

Evaluation Metrics

Model performance is evaluated using multiple metrics to capture different aspects of credit risk prediction. ROC-AUC measures ranking ability, KS statistic evaluates separation power, while recall and precision focus on default detection accuracy.

Result Interpretation

Experimental results show that Indian credit data exhibits stronger linear separability, making Logistic Regression a competitive and stable choice. In contrast, US credit data demonstrates higher complexity and policy sensitivity, where ensemble models such as Random Forest achieve superior performance.

These findings indicate that model effectiveness depends not only on algorithm selection but also on the underlying economic and financial structure of the data.

Limitations and Future Work

  • Models are trained on historical data and may require recalibration under changing economic conditions.
  • Explainability techniques such as SHAP and LIME can be integrated to improve model transparency and trust.
  • An end-to-end loan decision system can be developed by combining prediction models with business rules.
